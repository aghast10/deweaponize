# De-weaponize

Detect and disarm the following relational frames:

Opposition frames: language that constructs an adversarial relation between parties — combat metaphors, attack/defend positioning, zero-sum framing. The frame recruits the reader as audience to a fight. Reshape to disagreement or contrast: two parties with different positions, reader as observer.
Common triggers: slams, blasts, destroys, eviscerates, fires back, hits back, torches, rips, lashes out, goes after, takes aim, claps back, shuts down, doubles down, wages war on, battles, crushes, demolishes, targets, strikes.
Examples: "Senator slams bill" → "Senator opposes bill". "CEO fires back at critics" → "CEO responds to criticism". "X destroys Y in debate" → "X challenges Y's position". "Party wages war on regulation" → "Party seeks to reduce regulation".

Hierarchy frames: language that ranks parties morally — villain/victim/hero casting, moral authority claims, judgment from above. Flatten to behavioral description without ranking.
Common triggers: failed, disgraced, embattled, beleaguered, scandal-plagued, controversial, divisive, troubled, under fire, fallen, shamed, exposed, unfit, dangerous, reckless, irresponsible, heroic, brave, courageous (when used to frame moral standing rather than describe action).
Examples: "Disgraced former minister" → "Former minister". "The embattled CEO" → "The CEO". "Reckless policy decision" → "Policy decision that [specific consequence]". "Failed leadership" → "Leadership that did not achieve X". "Brave whistleblower" → "Whistleblower".

Comparison frames: language that establishes superiority/inferiority through loaded contrast. Reshape to parallel description.
Common triggers: unlike, while X succeeds Y fails, in contrast to the competent, dwarfs, puts to shame, makes X look like, outshines, leaves X in the dust, towers over.
Examples: "Unlike the competent X, Y stumbled" → "X achieved A; Y did not achieve B". "Their response dwarfs the government's" → "Their response was larger than the government's".

Causal/blame frames: language that concentrates causal responsibility beyond what facts establish. Reshape to behavioral description with proportional attribution.
Common triggers: caused, sparked, triggered, unleashed, plunged, drove X into, is to blame for, is responsible for, brought about, inflicted, engineered, orchestrated, masterminded, sabotaged.
Examples: "X caused the crisis" → "The crisis followed X's decision to [specific action]". "Policy plunged the country into recession" → "Recession began after the policy took effect". "CEO engineered the company's downfall" → "The company declined during the CEO's tenure".

Deictic/perspective frames: language that pulls the reader from observer into participant or target. Reposition to informed distance.
Common triggers: you need to know, this affects you, we must, our nation, wake up, pay attention, brace yourself, what you're not being told, they don't want you to know, this changes everything, here's why you should care, here's why that matters.
Examples: "Brace yourself for higher prices" → "Prices are expected to increase". "What they don't want you to know" → "[factual description of the information]". "This changes everything" → "[specific consequence]".

Alarm and threat frames on routine events: language that constructs threat relations where the facts describe ordinary situations.
Common triggers: malas noticias, no es normal, alerta, aviso, sorpresa, bad news for, threat, danger, warning, crisis (applied to non-crisis events), nightmare, chaos, out of control, terrifying.
Examples: "Bad news for drivers this weekend" → "Road closures expected this weekend". "Alerta por lluvias en Madrid" → "Se esperan lluvias intensas en Madrid". "Nightmare commute ahead" → "Significant delays expected". "Chaos at airports" → "Long queues at airports".

Superlative and exceptionalism frames: language that inflates scale to manufacture urgency before the reader can assess.
Common triggers: unprecedented, historic, never before seen, record-breaking, catastrophic, devastating, worst ever, earth-shattering, groundbreaking, game-changing, monumental, seismic, explosive, bombshell, shocking, stunning, jaw-dropping.
Examples: "Unprecedented crisis" → "Significant crisis" or "The largest since [reference]". "Bombshell revelation" → "Disclosure". "Jaw-dropping numbers" → "Notable figures". "Stunning defeat" → "Decisive defeat" or "Clear defeat".

For war and armed conflict: apply maximum frame reduction while preserving every fact. Combat-as-spectacle frames (score-keeping, game metaphors, storm/advance language) construct the reader as audience to entertainment. Aggressor/victim binaries recruit emotional allegiance. Dehumanizing collective labels erase individual agency. Reshape all of these: report what happened, to whom, where, without the relational scaffolding that tells the reader how to feel.
Common triggers: crushed, annihilated, wiped out, obliterated, pounded, hammered, stormed, seized, captured (used as scorekeeping), overran, enemy forces, the enemy, terrorists (as blanket label), regime, evil, barbaric, savages, death toll climbs (as dramatic tally), bloodbath, massacre (when unverified).
Examples: "Forces stormed the city" → "Forces entered the city". "Death toll climbs to 200" → "200 people have died". "Enemy forces obliterated" → "Opposing forces suffered significant losses". "Terrorist stronghold" → "Area controlled by [group name]".
